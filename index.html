PHASE 01
Foundations
0 / 7
▼
Before you look at a single deal. Get your infrastructure in place - mindset, entity, and market focus - or everything that follows is built on sand.
Terminology to Know
Investor Mindset
You are buying math, not a house. Emotions lose money. If you love the property, you're already in trouble.
Deal Flow
Your consistent pipeline of potential properties. No deal flow = no business. This is built deliberately, not stumbled into.
Entity Structure
Most investors buy inside an LLC, not personally. Provides liability protection and tax benefits. Talk to a CPA and attorney before Deal 1.
W-2 Investor
Industry term for someone flipping while still employed. Lenders and the IRS both treat you differently. Know this upfront.
Target Market
Your specific city, zip codes, and price range. Trying to flip everywhere = flipping nowhere. Narrow focus wins.
REIA
Real Estate Investors Association. Local meetup groups, usually free. Where you meet wholesalers, lenders, and contractors. Non-negotiable - join one immediately.

PHASE 02
Funding First
0 / 6
▼
Know your buying power before you ever look at a deal. A wholesaler sends you a deal on Tuesday. If you don't have a Proof of Funds letter and can't move fast, it's gone by Wednesday - and you burned the relationship.
Terminology to Know
Hard Money Lender
Lends based on the deal and property value, not your W-2 or credit score. Fast (7–14 days to close). Short term (6–18 months). Expensive: 10–14% interest, 2–4 points. Primary financing tool for flippers without their own cash.
Points
Upfront loan fees charged at closing. 1 point = 1% of loan amount. 2 points on a $150K loan = $3,000 out of pocket at closing. Model this in every deal.
LTV
Loan-to-Value. Loan amount as a % of the property's current as-is value. Most HMLs lend 65–75% LTV on purchase.
ARV-Based Lending
Some HMLs lend up to 65–70% of After Repair Value (not current value). Can cover both purchase and rehab. Most beginner-friendly lenders operate this way.
Draw Schedule
How rehab money gets released. HMLs don't hand you all the rehab cash upfront. They release it in stages after inspecting completed work milestones. You may need some cash to start before the first draw hits.
Proof of Funds (POF)
Letter from your HML confirming you're pre-approved and can close. Required to make credible offers. Get this before making a single offer.
Private Money Lender
An individual (friend, colleague, family) who lends at an agreed rate. Cheaper and more flexible than hard money. Start building these relationships at your REIA now - they become your biggest edge by Deal 2 or 3.
Extension Fee
If your project runs over schedule, HMLs charge a fee to extend the loan. Budget for this possibility in every deal.
Reality Check: 12% interest + 3 points on a $150K loan held 6 months ≈ $13,500 in financing costs out of your profit. The faster you renovate and sell, the less you bleed. A slow rehab is a profit killer.

PHASE 03
Comping & Underwriting
0 / 6
▼
Learn the math cold before deals start coming in. A good wholesaler deal has a 24–48 hour window. If you're figuring out how to comp while the clock ticks, you'll overpay or miss it. Practice on 20–50 fake deals first.
Terminology to Know
ARV
After Repair Value. What the property will be worth after all renovations are complete and it's in retail condition. Your single most important number - and the hardest to get right as a beginner.
Comp
A recently sold, similar property used to determine ARV. Must match: same neighborhood, similar sqft (within 15–20%), similar beds/baths, sold within 6 months, similar post-renovation condition.
CMA
Comparative Market Analysis. A formal comp report pulled from the MLS by an agent. More reliable than Zillow. Have your investor-friendly agent run one on every deal you get serious about.
70% Rule
Never pay more than 70% of ARV minus repair costs. (ARV × 0.70) − Repairs = MAO. Conservative by design - your starting filter, not a profit guarantee.
MAO
Maximum Allowable Offer. The highest price you can pay and still make the deal work. Know this number before you ever make an offer. Non-negotiable.
Holding Costs
Expenses that accumulate while you own the property: loan interest, property taxes, insurance, utilities, HOA. Every month you hold = money leaving your pocket.
Acquisition Costs
Costs to buy: closing costs, title insurance, transfer taxes. Budget 1–3% of purchase price.
Disposition Costs
Costs to sell: agent commissions (5–6%), closing costs, seller concessions. Budget 8–10% of ARV.
Contingency Budget
Extra money for unknown problems. Add 10–15% on top of every repair estimate. Always. No exceptions.
DOM
Days on Market. How long a comp took to sell. If comps are sitting 90+ days, your ARV may be optimistic. Fast DOM = healthy market.
The Underwriting Formula
ARV
− Repairs + Contingency (10–15%)
− Acquisition Costs (~2%)
− Holding Costs (interest + taxes + insurance × months)
− Disposition Costs (~8–10% of ARV)
− Financing Costs (points + rate × months)
─────────────────────────────
= Net Profit

Net Profit ÷ Total Cash Out of Pocket = ROI

PHASE 04
Finding Deals
0 / 6
▼
Now you're ready. You have POF, you know your MAO formula, and you can run a deal in under an hour. When a wholesaler sends you something, you're dangerous. Without Phases 2 and 3 done first, you're just a tourist.
Terminology to Know
MLS
Multiple Listing Service. Database of publicly listed properties. Most profitable flip deals are not here - or if they are, they're gone within hours. Good for comping practice though.
Off-Market Deal
A property not publicly listed. Owner hasn't listed it yet or doesn't want to. Best margins live here.
Wholesaler
An investor who finds distressed properties, puts them under contract, then sells that contract to you for an assignment fee. They do the hunting; you pay a premium. Good wholesalers become your most important deal source.
Assignment Fee
What the wholesaler charges you to hand over their contract. Typically $5K–$20K. Comes directly out of your profit - always factor it into your MAO.
Motivated Seller
An owner who needs to sell more than they need top dollar. The emotional driver behind every good deal. Situations: probate, divorce, pre-foreclosure, tired landlord, inherited property.
Pre-Foreclosure
Owner is behind on payments but hasn't lost the home yet. Public record. Consistently motivated sellers.
Probate
Property inherited after a death, sold by heirs who just want it liquidated. Frequently underpriced. Great consistent lead source.
Absentee Owner
Owns the property but doesn't live there. Often a tired landlord with high motivation to sell at a discount.
PropStream / BatchLeads
Software tools (~$100–150/mo) that pull distressed property lists, owner contact info, equity data, and foreclosure status. Essential for sourcing your own leads.
Skip Tracing
Finding contact info for a property owner using their name and address. Used to reach owners of off-market properties directly.
Driving for Dollars
Driving neighborhoods looking for distressed homes: overgrown yards, boarded windows, stacked mail. Old school but still works.
Investor-Friendly Agent
An agent who understands investor math, will write low offers without hesitation, and brings you deals before they hit the MLS. Critical to have one in your market.
The Hard Truth About Wholesalers: Many overprice their deals. Run your own numbers every time. A wholesaler deal must still pass the 70% rule after the assignment fee is added to your cost. Close fast, pay on time, and the best ones will feed you their best deals first.

PHASE 05
Managing the Rehab
0 / 9
▼
Where most beginners lose control of their profit. Your construction background is a serious edge here - use it. Your job is project manager, not laborer (mostly).
Terminology to Know
Scope of Work (SOW)
Your written, line-item bible for the entire rehab. Every task, every material, every cost. This is your contract protection and budget control. Never hire a contractor without a detailed, signed SOW.
General Contractor (GC)
Oversees the project, manages subs, pulls permits. You pay them 10–20% overhead on top of labor and materials. Your construction background may let you act as your own GC on Deal 1 - a significant cost savings.
Lien Waiver
Document a contractor signs confirming they've been paid and waiving their right to file a lien. Get one signed before every single payment. Non-negotiable.
Mechanic's Lien
A contractor's legal claim against your property for unpaid work. It clouds title and can kill your sale entirely. Lien waivers are your shield.
Draw Request
Your formal request to your HML to release a rehab disbursement after completing a defined work milestone.
Punch List
Final list of small incomplete items at the end of a project. Do not release final payment until every item is resolved.
Cosmetic vs. Gut Rehab
Cosmetic: Paint, flooring, fixtures, kitchen/bath updates. Fast, predictable. Your first deal should be this. Full gut: Structural, plumbing, electrical, HVAC to the studs. Much higher risk. Not for Deal 1.
Over-Improving
Spending more on finishes than the market will pay back. Granite countertops in a $130K neighborhood don't return granite-level value. Match the neighborhood standard - don't exceed it.
Contractor Red Flags - Walk away if you see: Wants 50%+ upfront · No license or insurance · No written contract · Can start immediately with no other work · Won't provide references from recent jobs.

PHASE 06
The Exit
0 / 7
▼
Get out at the right price, the right way. Understand this before you buy - but study it after you've absorbed everything else.
Terminology to Know
Disposition
The process of selling the finished property. Your exit in fix and flip is almost always a retail sale to an end buyer.
List Price Strategy
Pricing slightly below the top comp creates urgency and multiple offers. Pricing at the ceiling means slower sales and mounting holding costs.
Seller Concessions
Closing cost credits you offer the buyer. Buyers routinely ask for 1–3%. Budget for this in your original underwrite - not as a surprise later.
Appraisal Contingency
Buyer's lender appraises the property. If it comes in below contract price, you reduce price or the deal dies. Your comps must support the ARV - this is why accurate comping matters from Day 1.
Inspection Contingency
Buyer hires an inspector who may request repairs or credits. On a properly renovated flip this should be minor. Do not cut corners - they will be found.
Cash Buyer
No financing contingency, no appraisal, closes in 7–14 days. May offer slightly less - but the certainty often makes it worth it, especially on your first deal.
Plan B Exit
Before you buy any property, ask: if I can't sell at target price, could I rent it? What would it cash flow? Know your fallback before you close.
Net Proceeds
What you actually walk away with after paying off the hard money loan, agent commissions, closing costs, and concessions. This is your real profit number.

BONUS
Things Most Beginners Never Hear
▼
The money is made at purchase. You cannot negotiate your way to profit after you close. Buy right or don't buy.
Your first deal will take longer and cost more than planned. Budget for this mentally and financially before you start.
Wholesaler math is not your math. Their repair estimates are almost always low. Their ARVs are almost always optimistic. Run your own numbers every single time. No exceptions.
Hard money is a tool, not free money. The clock starts at closing. A slow permit, bad contractor, or delayed draw costs real dollars every single day.
Permits matter - especially electrical. Unpermitted work can blow up a deal in escrow. Your electrician background is a huge asset here - use it the right way and pull the permit.
Never be the most expensive house on the block. Over-improving is one of the most common beginner mistakes. Match the neighborhood - don't exceed it.
A reliable contractor who answers the phone is worth more than saving $500 on a bid. Contractor relationships are your #1 operational asset.
Know your buyer pool. Who actually buys in your target zip codes? Their preferences should drive every finish selection you make.
Side hustle pace means longer holds - price that in. If you can only check the job site a few times a week, your timeline extends. That's more holding cost. Model it honestly.
Track everything. Every receipt, every invoice, every mile driven. Your CPA needs it - and your true profit number depends on it.
